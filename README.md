# 💮 88. The Author’s Earnest Cry and Prayer by *Robert Burns*

<p>
    YE Irish lords, ye knights an’ squires,<br/>Wha represent our brughs an’ shires,<br/>An’ doucely manage our affairs<br/>                    In parliament,<br/>To you a simple poet’s pray’rs<br/>                    Are humbly sent.<br/><br/><br/>Alas! my roupit Muse is hearse!<br/>Your Honours’ hearts wi’ grief ’twad pierce,<br/>To see her sittin on her arse<br/>                    Low i’ the dust,<br/>And scriechinh out prosaic verse,<br/>                    An like to brust!<br/><br/><br/>Tell them wha hae the chief direction,<br/>Scotland an’ me’s in great affliction,<br/>E’er sin’ they laid that curst restriction<br/>                    On aqua-vit&æ;<br/>An’ rouse them up to strong conviction,<br/>                    An’ move their pity.<br/><br/><br/>Stand forth an’ tell yon Premier youth<br/>The honest, open, naked truth:<br/>Tell him o’ mine an’ Scotland’s drouth,<br/>                    His servants humble:<br/>The muckle deevil blaw you south<br/>                    If ye dissemble!<br/><br/><br/>Does ony great man glunch an’ gloom?<br/>Speak out, an’ never fash your thumb!<br/>Let posts an’ pensions sink or soom<br/>                    Wi’ them wha grant them;<br/>If honestly they canna come,<br/>                    Far better want them.<br/><br/><br/>In gath’rin votes you were na slack;<br/>Now stand as tightly by your tack:<br/>Ne’er claw your lug, an’ fidge your back,<br/>                    An’ hum an’ haw;<br/>But raise your arm, an’ tell your crack<br/>                    Before them a’.<br/><br/><br/>Paint Scotland greetin owre her thrissle;<br/>Her mutchkin stowp as toom’s a whissle;<br/>An’ d—mn’d excisemen in a bussle,<br/>                    Seizin a stell,<br/>Triumphant crushin’t like a mussel,<br/>                    Or limpet shell!<br/><br/><br/>Then, on the tither hand present her—<br/>A blackguard smuggler right behint her,<br/>An’ cheek-for-chow, a chuffie vintner<br/>                    Colleaguing join,<br/>Picking her pouch as bare as winter<br/>                    Of a’ kind coin.<br/><br/><br/>Is there, that bears the name o’ Scot,<br/>But feels his heart’s bluid rising hot,<br/>To see his poor auld mither’s pot<br/>                    Thus dung in staves,<br/>An’ plunder’d o’ her hindmost groat<br/>                    By gallows knaves?<br/><br/><br/>Alas! I’m but a nameless wight,<br/>Trode i’ the mire out o’ sight?<br/>But could I like Montgomeries fight,<br/>                    Or gab like Boswell, 2<br/>There’s some sark-necks I wad draw tight,<br/>                    An’ tie some hose well.<br/><br/><br/>God bless your Honours! can ye see’t—<br/>The kind, auld cantie carlin greet,<br/>An’ no get warmly to your feet,<br/>                    An’ gar them hear it,<br/>An’ tell them wi’a patriot-heat<br/>                    Ye winna bear it?<br/><br/><br/>Some o’ you nicely ken the laws,<br/>To round the period an’ pause,<br/>An’ with rhetoric clause on clause<br/>                    To mak harangues;<br/>Then echo thro’ Saint Stephen’s wa’s<br/>                    Auld Scotland’s wrangs.<br/><br/><br/>Dempster, 3 a true blue Scot I’se warran’;<br/>Thee, aith-detesting, chaste Kilkerran; 4<br/>An’ that glib-gabbit Highland baron,<br/>                    The Laird o’ Graham; 5<br/>An’ ane, a chap that’s damn’d aulfarran’,<br/>                    Dundas his name: 6<br/><br/><br/>Erskine, a spunkie Norland billie; 7<br/>True Campbells, Frederick and Ilay; 8<br/>An’ Livistone, the bauld Sir Willie; 9<br/>                    An’ mony ithers,<br/>Whom auld Demosthenes or Tully<br/>                    Might own for brithers.<br/><br/><br/>See sodger Hugh, 10 my watchman stented,<br/>If poets e’er are represented;<br/>I ken if that your sword were wanted,<br/>                    Ye’d lend a hand;<br/>But when there’s ought to say anent it,<br/>                    Ye’re at a stand.<br/><br/><br/>Arouse, my boys! exert your mettle,<br/>To get auld Scotland back her kettle;<br/>Or faith! I’ll wad my new pleugh-pettle,<br/>                    Ye’ll see’t or lang,<br/>She’ll teach you, wi’ a reekin whittle,<br/>                    Anither sang.<br/><br/><br/>This while she’s been in crankous mood,<br/>Her lost Militia fir’d her bluid;<br/>(Deil na they never mair do guid,<br/>                    Play’d her that pliskie!)<br/>An’ now she’s like to rin red-wud<br/>                    About her whisky.<br/><br/><br/>An’ Lord! if ance they pit her till’t,<br/>Her tartan petticoat she’ll kilt,<br/>An’durk an’ pistol at her belt,<br/>                    She’ll tak the streets,<br/>An’ rin her whittle to the hilt,<br/>                    I’ the first she meets!<br/><br/><br/>For God sake, sirs! then speak her fair,<br/>An’ straik her cannie wi’ the hair,<br/>An’ to the muckle house repair,<br/>                    Wi’ instant speed,<br/>An’ strive, wi’ a’ your wit an’ lear,<br/>                    To get remead.<br/><br/><br/>Yon ill-tongu’d tinkler, Charlie Fox,<br/>May taunt you wi’ his jeers and mocks;<br/>But gie him’t het, my hearty cocks!<br/>                    E’en cowe the cadie!<br/>An’ send him to his dicing box<br/>                    An’ sportin’ lady.<br/><br/><br/>Tell you guid bluid o’ auld Boconnock’s, 11<br/>I’ll be his debt twa mashlum bonnocks,<br/>An’ drink his health in auld Nance Tinnock’s 12<br/>                    Nine times a-week,<br/>If he some scheme, like tea an’ winnocks,<br/>                    Was kindly seek.<br/><br/><br/>Could he some commutation broach,<br/>I’ll pledge my aith in guid braid Scotch,<br/>He needna fear their foul reproach<br/>                    Nor erudition,<br/>Yon mixtie-maxtie, queer hotch-potch,<br/>                    The Coalition.<br/><br/><br/>Auld Scotland has a raucle tongue;<br/>She’s just a devil wi’ a rung;<br/>An’ if she promise auld or young<br/>                    To tak their part,<br/>Tho’ by the neck she should be strung,<br/>                    She’ll no desert.<br/><br/><br/>And now, ye chosen Five-and-Forty,<br/>May still you mither’s heart support ye;<br/>Then, tho’a minister grow dorty,<br/>                    An’ kick your place,<br/>Ye’ll snap your gingers, poor an’ hearty,<br/>                    Before his face.<br/><br/><br/>God bless your Honours, a’ your days,<br/>Wi’ sowps o’ kail and brats o’ claise,<br/>In spite o’ a’ the thievish kaes,<br/>                    That haunt St. Jamie’s!<br/>Your humble poet sings an’ prays,<br/>                    While Rab his name is.<br/><br/><br/>POSTSCRIPTLET half-starv’d slaves in warmer skies<br/>See future wines, rich-clust’ring, rise;<br/>Their lot auld Scotland ne’re envies,<br/>                    But, blythe and frisky,<br/>She eyes her freeborn, martial boys<br/>                    Tak aff their whisky.<br/><br/><br/>What tho’ their Phoebus kinder warms,<br/>While fragrance blooms and beauty charms,<br/>When wretches range, in famish’d swarms,<br/>                    The scented groves;<br/>Or, hounded forth, dishonour arms<br/>                    In hungry droves!<br/><br/><br/>Their gun’s a burden on their shouther;<br/>They downa bide the stink o’ powther;<br/>Their bauldest thought’s a hank’ring swither<br/>                    To stan’ or rin,<br/>Till skelp—a shot—they’re aff, a’throw’ther,<br/>                    To save their skin.<br/><br/><br/>But bring a Scotchman frae his hill,<br/>Clap in his cheek a Highland gill,<br/>Say, such is royal George’s will,<br/>                    An’ there’s the foe!<br/>He has nae thought but how to kill<br/>                    Twa at a blow.<br/><br/><br/>Nae cauld, faint-hearted doubtings tease him;<br/>Death comes, wi’ fearless eye he sees him;<br/>Wi’bluidy hand a welcome gies him;<br/>                    An’ when he fa’s,<br/>His latest draught o’ breathin lea’es him<br/>                    In faint huzzas.<br/><br/><br/>Sages their solemn een may steek,<br/>An’ raise a philosophic reek,<br/>An’ physically causes seek,<br/>                    In clime an’ season;<br/>But tell me whisky’s name in Greek<br/>                    I’ll tell the reason.<br/><br/><br/>Scotland, my auld, respected mither!<br/>Tho’ whiles ye moistify your leather,<br/>Till, whare ye sit on craps o’ heather,<br/>                    Ye tine your dam;<br/>Freedom an’ whisky gang thegither!<br/>                    Take aff your dram!<br/><br/><br/> Note 1. This was written before the Act anent the Scotch distilleries, of session 1786, for which Scotland and the author return their most grateful thanks.—R. B. [back]<br/>Note 2. James Boswell of Auchinleck, the biographer of Johnson. [back]<br/>Note 3. George Dempster of Dunnichen. [back]<br/>Note 4. Sir Adam Ferguson of Kilkerran, Bart. [back]<br/>Note 5. The Marquis of Graham, eldest son of the Duke of Montrose. [back]<br/>Note 6. Right Hon. Henry Dundas, M. P. [back]<br/>Note 7. Probably Thomas, afterward Lord Erskine. [back]<br/>Note 8. Lord Frederick Campbell, second brother of the Duke of Argyll, and Ilay Campbell, Lord Advocate for Scotland, afterward President of the Court of Session. [back]<br/>Note 9. Sir Wm. Augustus Cunningham, Baronet, of Livingstone. [back]<br/>Note 10. Col. Hugh Montgomery, afterward Earl of Eglinton. [back]<br/>Note 11. Pitt, whose grandfather was of Boconnock in Cornwall. [back]<br/>Note 12. A worthy old hostess of the author’s in Mauchline, where he sometimes studies politics over a glass of gude auld Scotch Drink.—R. B. [back]
</p>
